description: Among other items of interest, General Hamilton decries the spirit of faction that, especially in Virginia, is more violent than ever. The leaders there possess all powers of the local government
  and seem determined to possess those of the National as well, according to Hamilton. Hamilton is referring to the Jeffersonian opposition to Federalist policy. Also bemoans the "Perverseness and capriciousness"
  of President Adams. Blames the rising opposition on "Vanity and Jealousy," and writes that "Passion wrests the helm from reason." Concludes that the Federalist government is "in a sad Dilemma," although
  he is pleased that the Federalists have a "decided Majority" in Congress, and believes that "Good Will towards the Government... predominates" among the "Mass of the People." Hamilton is also pleased
  that "Sympathy with the French Revolution" seems to be less.
