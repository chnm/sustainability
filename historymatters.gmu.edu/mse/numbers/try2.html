Census records can tell you something about the past, and so can non-quantitative evidence, like newspaper accounts. What happens if you combine the two? Here’s an example from New York City in the 1850s, when the city was developing plans for Central Park, plans that required the displacement of a community known as Seneca Village.

Read the following excerpt from "The Present Look of Our Great Central Park," New-York Daily Times, July 9, 1865, then read a page from the 1855 census.

". . . West of the reservoir, within the limits of the Central Park, lies a neat little settlement, known as "Nigger Village." The Ebon inhabitants, after whom the village is called, present a pleasing contrast in their habits and the appearance of their dwellings to the Celtic occupants, in common with hogs and goats, of the shanties in the lower part of the Park. They have been notified to remove by the first of August. The policemen find it difficult to persuade them out of the idea which has possessed their simple minds, that the sole object of the authorities in making the Park is to procure their expulsion form the homes which they occupy. It is to be hoped that their removal will be effected with as much gentleness as possible. . . . In the southern portion of the Park limits there are several swamps and pools of stagnated water, from which a nauseous odor arises. The expense of draining these low lands will be felt by the tax-payers. If some of the hogs, goats, and other inmates of the shanties in this vicinity do not die of the yellow fever this Summer, it will only be because Death himself hesitates to enter such dirty hovels."

Now look at this census data from the 22nd Ward, New York City (part of the area that would become Central Park) in 1855. How does it support or challenge the conclusions drawn by the author of the newspaper account you just read?
