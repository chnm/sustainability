Phyllis Costley: This is a picture that I have on my wall in my living room. Wednesday, September 3 rd 1958, and these are the Negro students seeking admission to white Arlington schools outside the Alexandria Court House. And we went down to the Alexandria Court House for about a week or so, to get Judge Bryan to give us a ruling. And I have three children in this picture: Lewis, Melvin, and Sandra. And some of the parents are in here too, of the kid. I’m not on there. Dorothy Hamm is not on there, cause we were out there doing something else, running our mouths with some of the lawyers I imagine, which is what we were always doing.

Dorothy Hamm: We were back in court. We had five of the original students. And 25 additional students’ names were added to the list. Some of these 25 students were students from South Arlington. However, each of the 30 students were denied admission because of criteria set up by the School Board. The first was attendance area. The second was it was overcrowded at W-L. The third was academic accomplishment. The fourth was psychological problems. And adaptability, was more or else used because the school board felt that they would not adjust or adapt to a change in school or a mixture with white children, or that type of thing. And one of the things that was used against my son, which really upset me, was that when I raised the question that he wouldn’t be loved, that really disturbed me, because I was sending him to school not only to be loved but to be taught.
