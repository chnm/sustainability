This is Dr. Ben Page's longest course of consecutive treatment for the Mathews family. In the course of twelve housecalls he performed four bleedings on Mrs. Mathews. It seems as if this is inconsistent with the claim made in the Dr. Ben Page Memorial Tribute (recall TASK 1) that he disapproved of bleeding patients. However, we do not have enough information to draw this conclusion without answering further questions. For instance, we would need to know how often other doctors bled patients in comparison with Dr. Page. And whether or not he bled his other patients as frequently as he did Mrs. Mathews. Answers to these questions would help determine the accuracy of the Ben Page Memorial Tribute.

The fevers Dr. Ben Page and Martha Ballard treated were often life threatening and reached epidemic proportions. Martha Ballard treated scarlet fever which she called 'canker rash,' 'puerperal fever,' and 'bilious fever.' She lost three of her children to a scarlet fever epidemic. In the course of his career, Ben Page treated spotted fever, smallpox, plague, and bilious fever. Page himself had smallpox, and was an early experimenter with the smallpox vaccine.

The therapies doctors and female healers used to combat fevers were similar, in part, because each subscribed to the theory of humors which held that poor health was caused by the imbalance of four bodily fluids. Both doctors and female healers used various "pukes and purges" to treat imbalances of the humors. The doctors at that time also believed in 'solidistic theory' which was loosely based on Newtonian physics. Believers of this theory sought to regulate the mechanical properties of 'solids.' To this end, doctors employed tonics to stimulate bodily force and sedatives to induce relaxation. Therapies and theories were used in combination with one another.

Newspaper ads can be as useful to the historical detective as newspaper articles. It is interesting to read down the list of drugs Dr. Page was selling. Most were imported and Martha Ballard occaisionally a few. But most of what she used came from her own herb garden or from the forests and fields nearby.

Letters of correspondence are informal exchanges of ideas, advice, and opinions that are usually written with the intention of being read only by the addressee. Dr. Vaughan's letters to Dr. Page show how knowledge of medical treatments was disseminated among colleagues in an age without universal standards in health care.

You can find letters of correspondence in your local library, in special collections at schools and universities and often stored in family attics and basements. They can cover topics as diverse as romance to cutting edge developments in science, to describing the progress of battles and other military matters.
