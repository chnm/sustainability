WHAT ADVICE WOULD YOU GIVE STUDENTS ON ANALYZING SPEECHES?

I guess first thing that I would do is talk to students about the 19th-century voice. And that the 19th-century voice is really quite different from the 20th-century voice. And that it takes a while to get used to it. Maybe they never will. But, not to go and expect to find a 20th-century voice. So kind of the uniqueness of the 19th-century voice. If you're put off by it, if it seems excessive, and the last time I used this the students responded by saying "oh, its so Victorian." To see that and to acknowledge that it's difficult. And that it's not our voice. And then, I guess that would be the first step. And then after it would be to move beyond that and the idea that it's very unfamiliar. I guess that's what I'm trying to say. If you haven't done a lot of work in the 19th century, to say "This is an unfamiliar voice," and to acknowledge its unfamiliarity, I guess would be the first thing.

And then to move on from there and to say, "ok, well, what can I do with this unfamiliarity?" And just to, you know, read the passages over. To maybe look for the personal voice. You know, we all want to know "I" the speaker. What makes this Frances Ellen Watkins Harper's speech as opposed to anybody else's? But then to realize that part of the 19th-century voice is the omission of the "I" of the personal. And that Truth is in fact much more exceptional in that way than Harper. That it's very very hard to find any kind of personal voice or lines of personal experience in these 19th-century women. And that they were very determined to keep themselves, their private self, in the background. "That's not what we're about." There's a kind of reticence and this sense of privacy, which we've totally lost in the 20th century. But really kind of, my private business is my private business. And I'm here doing the public work of racial uplift, or of abolition, of anti-slavery, or whatever.
