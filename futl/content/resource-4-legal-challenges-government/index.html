Separate but not Equal in Arlington

In 1949, Oliver Hill and the NAACP filed suit, on behalf of Arlington teenager Constance Carter, against the School Board of Arlington County. Like many African American students in Arlington, Carter had attended school in Washington, DC. When Washington, D.C., initially began charging tuition for non-residents, Arlington paid the tuition. When Arlington ended this policy, students such as Carter could only attend Hoffman-Boston, a school that offered far fewer academic subjects than the whites-only high school, Washington-Lee.

In Carter v. School Board of Arlington County, Carter petitioned the court to force Arlington to provide equal facilities at Hoffman-Boston. District Judge Albert Bryan ruled that Arlington schools did not violate the “separate but equal” provision established in Plessy v. Ferguson. Bryan noted that Washington-Lee had more students so a larger facility was appropriate. The court also noted that while Washington-Lee offered courses such as “speech, journalism, solid geometry, commercial arithmetic, bookkeeping, automobile mechanics, woodworking, and printing,” Hoffman-Boston offered courses that Washington-Lee did not, such as brick-laying.

The Carter case shows that the NAACP effort to end legal segregation experience setbacks as well as victories. It also shows that courts accepted segregation as the law of the land. They often did not address the ways in which segregated high schools systematically denied students, such as Carter, the chance to take the subjects offered at Washington-Lee. Just a year after this case was decided, the NAACP introduced a case challenging the very idea that separate could be equal.

Part of Judge Bryan's reasoning in this decision was that not every student in Virginia had the right to attend a school with the facilities of Washington-Lee. How would you respond to this reasoning?
