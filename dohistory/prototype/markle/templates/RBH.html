"Reconsidering Birth and Healing" game segment
pathway for Dr. Page and Parthenia's autopsy (see Area E of Diagram B)


The following is a description of one pathway--Reconsidering Birth & Healing--for one segment of Martha's story--Dr. Page and Parthenia's Autopsy. This is one of four ways people can choose to look at the incidents in Martha's story surrounding her encounters with young Dr. Page, and the autopsy of her niece Parthenia (represented in the game diagram as AREA E). The other pathways for area E--"Doing History," "Looking at Public & Private Realms," and "Understanding Filmmaking"--will each approach the same events in Martha's life from very different perspectives and will seek to teach very different skills. Each segment of Martha's life (represented as Areas A-G in the game diagram) will have a varying numbers of the four possible pathways available to users, for a total of 21 different possible experiences of the segments of Martha's story explored in the Interactive Detective Game.

The Reconsidering Birth & Healing pathway compares feelings and conceptions about midwives and professional physicians from the eighteenth century to the present. The goal of this pathway is for users to come away understanding that:

1.  Although women are difficult to find in eighteenth-century historical records, they were the primary healers in Martha Ballard's time.
2.  Martha Ballard and those like her practiced "social medicine"; they were tied to their clients through social networks and traditional practices.
3.  In contrast, emerging professional physicians used ideas from the Enlightenment and the Scientific Revolution to define themselves as professionals and separate themselves from those they treated.
4.  Martha Ballard's era was also a period of transition toward full-time, formally-trained professional physicians.
5.  The ratios of male to female practitioners of these kinds of medicine changed over time in the United States. Today, male-female divisions in medical work have blurred, although they have not disappeared.

The Man-Midwife

Users see on the screen an image of the 1793 London cartoon titled "The Man--Mid--Wife."

A vertical line divides the picture into two equal halves. At the center of the cartoon, and also divided by the vertical line, is a half male, half female figure. The figure's right side is clothed in a fashionable dress and dust cap. She is a female midwife. The hearth behind her yields evidence of only simple tools of her vocation: a steaming pot, cloths drying on a line, a bottle on the mantle, a shallow scoop in her palm. The figure's right side is clothed as a gentleman. He is a male physician who attends births. A large mortar and pestle is in his corner, and three shelves of bottles and vials extend in back of him. Forceps and shears hang on the wall. He holds a probe-like instrument longer than his forearm.

Users access information about the items in this scene by clicking on them. For example, clicking on:

  •   "Man-Midwife" yields a look at period definitions of the word midwife. Other terms such as "Doctress", from Martha Ballard's diary, illustrate the transitional character of medicine and healing in Martha Ballard's time. An explanatory paragraph points out the mix of meanings in each phrase.
  •   the forceps yields a look at eighteenth and nineteenth-century English and American engravings of birth instruments used by physicians. Passages from medical texts explain how the instruments were used, including month-by-month illustrations of a developing fetus in utero, and illustrations of how to extract a baby's head using forceps. A short history of forceps follows.
  •   the mortar and pestle yields a discussion of how they were used to crush and prepare medicines.
  •   the cloths hanging in the hearth yields Martha Ballard's diary entries that mention rags as well as a short history of bandages from household rags to plastic strips.

    • The screen has additional buttons outside the perimeter of the picture. Following the paths behind these buttons allows the user to delve deeper into the issues raised in the Man--Mid--Wife illustration. Buttons down each side read Medicines, Picturing Healers & Physicians, Timeline, one of each on the left and right. At the center top margin is a button labeled Conflict and at the center bottom one labeled Congruence.

    Medicines

    By clicking on the female midwife's Medicines button the user sees several Martha Ballard diary entries that mention herbs and medicines that Martha made or used. Some diary entries show Martha growing or gathering herbs and some mention symptoms and ailments.

    For example: "Cut sage and feverfue. I made a decoction of feverfue, peneroil & Cammomile for my self & daughter" or "Mrs Nason calld to get some Dock root for the itch" or "old mrs Kenny advised to giv her a syrup of vinegar and onions, and a decoction of gold thread and shumake berries. It was done and she seemed revived."

    Clicking on the highlighted words (italicized in this example) brings up questions to resolve and provides relevant information for answering the questions. For example, clicking on the itch brings up the question, What was the itch and how was it treated? How would it be treated in the late twentieth century? The page offers options for continuing to drill down for information. The questions can be answered by using passages from the Ballard diary; from eighteenth-, nineteenth-, and twentieth-century medical texts; and descriptions and color illustrations of Dock root from Nicholas Culpepper's The Complete Herbal (1649 with many reprints). Users see that the ailment is probably scabies, and they read about people in the Ballard diary bathing for scabies before marriage. After attempting to answer the questions, users are returned to the original diary passage where they can double click on the same word to see the correct answers.

    Clicking on the physician's Medicines button brings up ads from the local paper in Hallowell, Maine. They read: "Dr. Benjamin Page has a very handsome assortment of Drugs and Medicine, among which are a variety of patent articles" including "Andersons, Hoopers, and Lockyers Pills, Batemans Drops, Turlingtons Balsam of Life, and Daffys Elixir."

    As in the female midwife's Medicines side, by clicking on the highlighted words (italicized in this example), users will find questions to resolve as well as information to resolve those questions. For example, What were patent medicines and how did doctors obtain and use them? Did professional doctors use herbal preparations? Sources such as medicine labels, medical texts, and letters from eighteenth-century doctors ordering medicines and compounds from importers help provide answers. Again, as in the other side, after attempting to answer the questions, users are returned to the original passage where they can double click on the same word to see the correct answers.

    Picturing Healers and Physicians

    By clicking on the physician's button labeled Picturing Healers and Physicians, users see a portrait of Dr. Philomen Tracy (c. 1790) holding the hand of a female patient that extends from a bed curtain. Only the hand of the patient is visible. Tracy fills most of the frame and is dressed in a black waistcoat with brass buttons, a green vest, and a white cravat. He holds a stoppered bottle in his left hand. Users are asked to note details of the picture: How does Philomen Tracy's clothing compare with that of the man-midwife? What could the vial be? Why is there only a hand of the patient showing, and why is it extending from a curtain? Clicking on details in the portrait gives information that can help answer the questions. For example, clicking on the hands reveals a text of the times explaining pulses and their diagnostic uses. Clicking on the curtain brings up an eighteenth-century Dutch print of a male midwife during a delivery. He sits at the feet of the propped-up mother in labor, with the bedsheet tied around his neck so that he cannot see below the mother's waist. After the user has attempted to answer the questions, they will be able to access the correct answers.

    Clicking on the More button on the Tracy portrait will reveal more eighteenth- and nineteenth-century portraits of male doctors. Several of these doctors, such as Drs. Coney and Page, lived in Martha Ballard's community of Hallowell, Maine. The users see and answer questions about these portraits. Users are asked to go to the button on the female midwife's side to compare what they see.

    The Picturing Healers and Physicians button on the female side yields a statement that no American images of midwives at work during Martha Ballard's time have been found. Under an empty frame, they read that no portraits of Martha Ballard exist. When users click on the empty frame a question appears: How can we fill in this picture?

    Images from early children's books and from the collection of the New England Antiquarian Society show unidentified people clustered around those who are ill or in childbirth. After seeing the images, users are asked to go through the other Picturing Healers and Physicians button, on the physicians side, if they have not already. Upon completion of both sides to this point, users see film footage in which the Philomen Tracy portrait is replicated by actors. A tight shot shows only what the portrait shows, and the image freezes. Users are asked, how many people are in this room, and who are they likely to be? After giving an answer, users can see the film resume and the camera pulls away to reveal a roomful of people.

    For comparison, a sixteenth-century French print of a birth attended by a midwife replaces the film and also shows many people present: the husband, the midwife, and a team of women supporting and attending the mother in labor.

    Diary entries from Martha Ballard indicate what other women are in attendance at a number of births in Hallowell. Clicking on Audio gives a short commentary by Laurel Ulrich in which she explains that while we can see what many male doctors looked like, the only image of Martha Ballard and her practice is from her diary. Ulrich proceeds to explain that midwives practiced a tradition of "social medicine" that included teams of women and helpers. She also explains that birth or serious disease was cause for people to gather in and around the birth room or sickroom in the eighteenth century.

    Timeline

    Clicking on the Timeline button on the male side presents a series of dates from the history of males in obstetrics. For example: William Smellie's improvement of forceps, the founding of the Maine Medical Society, the founding of the Boston Medical Society, the opening of Harvard Medical School, the dates when doctors begin officiating at births in Boston and Philadelphia, publication of the first American treatise on Midwifery, the years of practice of doctors mentioned by Martha Ballard, and numbers of male midwives, obstetricians, and trained nurses appearing in twentieth-century U.S. Census counts. Users can click on each to find out what each is and its significance to the practice of obstetrics. After users arrange the dates in chronological order, they create a timeline by choosing from options for time intervals, design forms, and items to include. A Merge button allows a parallel comparison or merging with dates from the female Timeline dates.

    A Timeline button on the female side of the Man-Midwife is constructed in a similar manner. Examples of the dates on this side include laws governing the testimony of mothers during childbirth, the years of practice of Martha Ballard, the first woman to enter an American medical school, the opening of the Boston and New York infirmaries for women, rules barring women from male medical schools, eighteenth-century publication of Sarah Stones The English Midwife, publication of Culpepper's The Compleat Herbal, publication of E. Smith's The Compleat Housewife, and numbers of female midwives, obstetricians, and trained nurses appearing in twentieth century U.S. Census counts. A Merge button allows a parallel comparison or merging with dates from the male Timeline dates.

    Conflict

    The single Conflict button in the top center of the screen represents points of conflict between midwives and physicians. It attempts to raise and answer the question, How did they get along in practice?
    Clicking on this button, the users see a sequence from the film A Midwife's Tale, when Dr. Benjamin Page is called in by a woman who panics during labor. He gives her 20 drops of laudenum (an opiate), which slows down her labor. He leaves, and, after the drug wears off, Martha successfully delivers the child. It is clear from Martha's diary entry that she does not approve of this upstart. Laurel Ulrich's voice-over tells us that Ben Page is a new breed of doctor. He is determined to make a full time practice of medicine and to come in during ordinary deliveries, not just in emergencies. Users are asked, What is the source of the conflict shown here? Who won and why?

    A women-and-men-in-medicine timeline, using selected items from the Timeline activities, stretches along the bottom of the next frame. Users click on highlighted markers on the timeline to see quotes illustrating other aspects of the conflict. For example,


    A 1737 excerpt from Sarah Stone's The Compleat Practice of Midwifery shows a point of argument on the practical use of dissections. The quote says, in part, "had I inspected into [dissections] all my life, and had not been instructed in Midwifery by my Mother, and Deputy to her full six years, it would have signified but little."

    An 1820 excerpt from a Harvard professor's treatise decries the possibility of training women to be midwives because dissections are essential to the study of medicine. He wrote: "many of our more delicate feelings, much of our refined sensibility must be subdued, before we can submit to the sort of discipline required in the study of medicine; in females they must be destroyed."


    Other excerpts trace arguments around women and men in medicine up to the present time, including women as physicians and men as nurses.

    After all the quotes have been read, the Man--Mid--Wife cartoon reappears, and this time the author's writing below is included. Users are asked to note the date of the cartoon and locate it on the timeline at the bottom of the screen. They see a typed transcript that reveals the author's intent to characterize the male midwife as a monster and animal. [The handwritten words on the cartoon itself are likewise transformed into typescript by clicking on them.] Now the artist's intended meaning becomes clear; the cartoon lambasts men who become midwives for lascivious reasons.

    Congruence

    Just as there were points of conflict between midwives and physicians, so were there points where their paths converged and merged. A click on the Congruence button brings up the obituary of Ben Page, one of the doctors in Martha Ballard's diary. The obituary praises Pages development of a "female sensibility" in his practice.

    An excerpt from the popular nineteenth-century Godey's Lady's Book argues for the acceptance of woman into male medical school in the United States.

    Excerpts from the diary of Clara Barton (Martha's niece) give a sense of her tasks as a Civil War nurse.

    Using the twentieth-century U.S. Census counts of male and female obstetricians, physicians, midwives, healers, trained nurses, and untrained nurses, users will create a series of graphs to see visually the relative trends toward more integration of the genders in medical job categories.
