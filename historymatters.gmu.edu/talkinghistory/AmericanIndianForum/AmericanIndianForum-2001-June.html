    I was moved by Barb Tracy's testiment to the responsibilities of
privilege (see below.) I have grown up among, and traveled among, very
humble peoples. They taught me more, and I owe them more, than the equally
good people behind my various degrees and certificates. Barb has stated this
well and carefully.
    Final thoughts: This seminar has continued, for me, that instruction. I
have had fortuitous contacts with, and friends among, the various Iroquois
nations, the Arapaho, and the Navajo. I have also had contact - much less
extended - with "tribals" in India, and peasant village peoples in Central
America, Southeast Asia and Japan.
    I am 66, with three grandchildren. I no longer have the time and
patience for the silliness of most academic ambitions, educational
institutional absurdities, or an increasingly ridiculous and obscene
marketplace. For me, this seminar dealt with some of the terrible realities
facing the world today, and worthy of extended discussion.
    Thank you, everyone. Include me in any new seminars. This has been an
honor for me. If those of us in a position of privilege are to stop
destroying the earth and turn to helping save it, we will need all of the
instruction we can get.

> everyone is privileged except for the very poorest people.  I do however

> gives us more privileges than those who don't.  We all have access to
> computers and the computer literacy to use them.  That gives us privilege
> over those who do not.  That makes no one better or worse than the other.
> It simply means that we have advantages over others.  What IS important

> communities and that we give back to those who give to us and that we do
> not forget or turn a blind eye on the many people who do not have computer

> everyone.  We forget that or we don't know it.  Again I highly recommend
> reading Cynthia Selfe's book.  Cynthia and I have been involved in internet
> education since the early 90's and clearly recognize the advantages it has
> given so many.  We have students who would not be able to go to school if
> it weren't for our on-line courses, but we are both clearly aware that we
> cannot reach everyone who wants an education because of the still existing
> limitations of technology.  And these limitations are not as minimal as one
> might believe.   Unless they have transportation, many Indian people living
> on reservations do not have access to the net.  I am not talking about the
> K-12 group who may or may not have computers in their schools.  I am
> talking about parents, adults, and the elderly on Pine Ridge for example
> who might not have cars and who would need to travel miles to the nearest
> internet connection.  I am not talking about gee let's all get in our cars
> and drive to small towns and teach them how to use e-mail and the web.
> That's already happening.
> Pine Ridge Reservation sets on over 2 million acres and has approximately
> 25,000 people living there. Many of the homes do not have running water or
> electricity.  6 out of 10 families do not have a phone.  I don't think
> there is a public library. There is one grocery store on the entire
> reservation and the nearest big city is 95 miles away.  Because of the
> severe poverty and high unemployment, many people do not have cars. So
> given the geography and lack of public transportation, hopping over to the
> local library would be tough.  Pine Ridge is also the poorest reservations

> understanding is that despite President Clinton's famedPine Ridge visit and

> Ridge, the computers available there for public use are old, used, and not
> maintained, and that many don't know how to use them, much less update and
> maintain them.  Also the system there is unreliable.
>

> everyone everywhere.  Glen Martin in his article for _Wired_ says that

> Imagine not having the ability to call for emergency medical
> assistance--what we take for granted when we pick up the phone and dial
> 911.
>
> You might also read Rory J. O'Connor's essay "Africa: The Unwired

> shocking.
>
> We might also add language to the discussion of access.  If your only
> language is one spoken by a small population or if you are unable to write
> in any language, how much use is the web?   Two years ago I was preparing
> to teach in Turkey.  I found not only is the web largely unavailable and
> unused in Turkey, but I also found very few web pages written by Turkish
> people living in Turkey.  Most of the pages written in the Turkish language
> were written by Turkish students at American universities.  On the other
> hand, I can find lots of web pages written in English about Turkey.   If I

> population, I wouldn't find the web of much use to me even if I had access.

> So is this a hopeless situation?  Is the glass half empty?  NO, there is so

Dear Friends:

    Just a few final responses to the end-of-the-trail posts.  I have
appreciated them as I have appreciated all of the contributions during the
month.  By my count we are ending at about 100 pp. of commentary. It makes an
interesting document.

1. The comments on politics and policy are very interesting although I
confess they make me feel a little old.  Of course politics is a central
theme in contemporary Native American life (how many leaders of other
American racial and ethnic minority groups can reel off the names of 19th
century Supreme Court decisions?). What I find  somewhat ironic is that over
the past generation historians have argued that Indians are not "only
politics" and that Indian history needs to embrace other topics: religion,
family, architecture, landscape, economic systems, gender and so on.  It is
absolutely proper to be reminded, however, that all of these theme occur in a
political context and that Native Americans have been defined by their
political status.

But does "politics transcend cultural diversity?" That would probably take
another month to resolve. I would only say that politics certainly affects
cultural diversity and establishes a context for cultural life. But
transcends? Perhaps in some communities--urban?--but I don't think I would
say everywhere. It is a great question.

2. I appreciated the responses to my query about women in community colleges,
but as an historian I guess I still think there are historical answers, or at
least historical contributions, to the answers to that question.  What I was
trying to suggest was that we have very few histories of gender and gender
construction--both male and female--in contemporary Native American
societies.  One could ask my question in reverse: why so few men?

3. Melissa Meyer's class exercise struck me as marvelous and I intend to use
it.  I have done something similar for many years. I go through the
room--takes a while if you have a big class--and ask every person to write
down the names of three Indians. I go through the room recording their
responses.  I put them all on the board (including tally marks for the number
of Crazy Horses and Pocahantas's I get)
I then say, "knowing this, what is the history of Native Americans?" When did
it start? What are its themes? etc.  We then step back and I ask them to
explain this response.  (Also like Melissa I say this is an exercise, a way
of taking stock of what is in our heads, not a quiz.)  It has been very
interesting. I have even used it when speaking to nonacademic audiences like
Rotary Clubs and so on.  It is nonthreatening but it makes a point: we need
to know more. Native students seem to appreciate it as well as non-natives.

But I like Melissa's focus on history and comparing Indians to a nation.

4. The Internet comments are also much appreciated. As are the
bibliographical references. There is always more out there than I realize.
Thanks for the helpl

And keep building those bridges!

Fred Hoxie
