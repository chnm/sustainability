American history is taken mostly by third year students who are about to graduate with their three year pass degrees or go on to a four year honours program; it has had an unusually high number of history majors (46-50 percent the last time taught), many of whom will become teachers, yet these students have often not done any American history. If they have done American history before, at high school, it is overwhelmingly post-1945 history, the Cold War and the Kennedy years, that they have studied. A small minority have done the 1920s and 1930s, and an even smaller number the Civil War. So there is the challenge of teaching good history students with a wide knowledge of history but little background. A survey seems the most viable way of doing this. Moreover, the interests of students in Australia varies widely and only a survey can give them the variety of knowledge they want. Many are interested largely in the race question; women students however are also interested in women’s history topics, and since the 1980s there has been a rising interest in the history of Native Americans (stimulated by American films). Because Australia is a society marked by multiculturalism and immigration, and many students come from Asian or European ethnic backgrounds, immigration and social mobility are also popular topics. Few students are interested in American politics, and they find the complexities of the American political system and the constitution to be quite mind-boggling. All feel familiar with American culture and watch American films, while at the same time being quite critical of the United States and its history.

Despite the predominance of Australian students, the course attracts a small group of American students each year. Most American students on study abroad in Australia want to and are encouraged to take courses in Australian Studies; some schools discourage American history from being taken abroad. However those who do the course report repeatedly that the interpretations differ sharply from the American history they have known, especially in the lectures. They enroll in this course specifically to get an Australian perspective on American history, and they report that they have found it.
