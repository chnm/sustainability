October 10, 1794

In bringing A Midwife's Tale to the screen, the filmmakers had to figure out how to make Martha Ballard's world three-dimensional -- and oftentimes this required venturing into areas where historians don't know the answers. The filmmakers worked closely with scholars, material culture experts, and medical practitioners to come up with convincing historical guesses that also worked dramatically.

This particular scene, based on the entry of October 10, 1794, required answering questions like:

  • how wealthy was this family and how might they have furnished their home? From probate records we know they were pretty well off, and their payment of 12 shillings to Martha -- double her usual fee -- was another clue. So the production designer chose a four-postered bed draped with imported, not homespun, fabric.

  • what were the accents of these people? There are no existing sound recordings and there were no Dr. Higgins-type philologists in Hallowell Maine 200 years ago. How did we decide on dialect? One of the our best clues to accent was Martha's own phonetic spelling (eg "dafter" for "daughter"). Another source of clues were rhymes in childrens' poems from the period.

  • what were Martha's medical practices? what did she actually do during a delivery? Her diary tells us very little. So we inferred from the practices of very successful modern-day midwives we spoke to and worked with. In this scene, Martha tells her patient to "get up and walk," which is what most modern midwives recommend -- using gravity rather than fighting it.

  • what instruments did Ben Page take to a delivery? The prop master looked at medical kits in a number of museum collections and decided Ben Page probably had most of the recently invented equipment but not all of it. A leathersmith was then hired to make a replica based on a real medical kit from the period.

    History of Medicine:

    http://www.nlm.nih.gov/
    The U.S. National Library of Medicine Web site contains images from the history of medicine as well as on-line Exhibits. Topics include medical education, cesaerian sections, tinctures and medicine shows.

    http://www.cl.utoledo.edu/canaday/quackery/quack-index.html
    This Web site is entitled, From Quackery to Bacteriology: The Emergence of Modern Medicine in 19th Century America -- An Exhibition. It traces the evolution of homeopathy and heroic medicine through the 19th Century. It summaries the 18th Century medical establishment and explains why the practices of healers and doctors came into conflict in the 19th Century.

    Estes, J. Worth. (1990) Dictionary of Protopharmacology: Therapeutic Practices, 1700-1850. Canton, MA

    Wertz, Richard W. and Wertz, Dorothy C.. (1977) Lying-In: A History of Childbirth in America. New York: Schocken.

    Leavitt, Judith Walzer. "'Science' Enters the Birthing Room: Obstetrics in America Since the Eighteenth Century," Journal of American History 70 (1983): 281-304.

    Ulrich, Laurel Thatcher. (1990) A Midwife's Tale: The Life of Martha Ballard, Based on Her Diary, 1785-1812. New York: Alfred A. Knopf.
