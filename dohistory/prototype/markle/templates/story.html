About Martha Ballard and her diary



Between 1785 and 1812, Maine midwife Martha Ballard kept a daily diary in Hallowell, Maine. Against all odds, her many small diary booklets survived. They were passed down through the family's female line to great-great-grandaughter Mary Hobart who inherited them in 1884, the same year she graduated from the Women's Medical College of the New York Infirmiry. Mary organized Martha's many little diary booklets and bound them into two large clothbound volumes which she donated to the Maine State Library shortly before she died. For more than half a century, the diary was overlooked or dismissed by scholars who considered it "full of trivia."

Shortly after getting her Ph.D. in the early 1980s, Laurel Thatcher Ulrich was on an unrelated research trip at the Maine State Library when she stumbled across Martha's massive but cryptic diary. For eight years she pieced together the surviving fragments dealing with the lives of the people in Martha's community, and interpreted them with remarkable historical skill and imagination to reveal an often startling, intriguing portrait of a woman -- and a world in upheaval. Her book A Midwife's Tale: The Life of Martha Ballard, Based on Her Diary 1785-1812 won the Pultizer and Bancroft prizes in 1991.
