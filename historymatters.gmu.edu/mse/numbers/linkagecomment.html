The documents provided here refute the descriptions of the Seneca Village residents as “vagabonds and scoundrels,” with “no reguar occupation,” “very little knowledge of the English language,” and living in “rickety shanties.” The portrait that emerges from the documents is of a stable family, living in New York for 40 years; owning property for at least 17 years that is valued at between $2,300 and $4,000; and with one member employed as a cartman. These documents also show the benefits of record linkage—of tracing individuals through multiple documents. If we only had access to Andrew Williams’ petition, we could know that he owned land, but we would not know that he had lived in the same place for nearly two decades or that he could read. Nor could we know that Seneca Village appears to have been a stable community with two African-American churches.

For more about Seneca Village see the New-York Historical Society’s Seneca Village Web site (http://projects.ilt.columbia.edu/seneca/start.html) and The Park and the People: A History of Central Park by Roy Rosenzweig and Elizabeth Blackmar (Cornell University Press, 1998).
