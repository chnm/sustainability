The letter also reveals Hammond’s logic of the unalterable servitude of these slaves: he reasoned (and no doubt genuinely believed) that it was "cruelty" to free his slaves "& send them North." He thus forecloses the idea of freeing his slaves and letting them remain in the South — South Carolina and other states sharply restricted the numbers and movement of free African Americans — and doubtless Hammond would not want his former possessions free and about. He also believed that it would be "cruel" to the slaves to free them; his self-fulfilling and paternalistic reasoning dictated that slaves were inferior and ill suited to freedom, so, for their own good, they must remain slaves.

To gain a wider context for Hammond’s justification of slavery, and that of other white slaveholders, you can begin by looking at some of Hammond’s public speeches defending and recommending slavery. For example, you could read his famous "mud sill" speech in the Senate in 1852 and compare its logic and assumptions to the contents of this letter. You can also consider the larger pro-slavery argument in national politics as it developed beginning in the 1840s and contrast it to the arguments of abolitionists, who were especially eager to point out the sexual exploitation of slave women by their masters and singled out the large numbers of mixed race children as the chief national shame of slavery.
