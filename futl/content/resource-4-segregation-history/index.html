Resource 4 Segregation (History)

Source:

Lynchings by states and counties in the United States, 1900-1931, New York, NY : American Map Company, c.1931 www.loc.gov/item/2006636636 (Accessed November 17, 2016).

Enforced with Violence

For African Americans who broke the written or unwritten rules of Jim Crow segregation the consequences could be deadly. Attacks on African Americans included lynchings and race riots. A lynching was a murder of one or multiple individuals carried out by a large group without a trial. Race riots were carried out by mobs of whites who attacked African Americans and often destroyed property in black neighborhoods.

Lynchings and race riots occurred throughout the North and South especially between 1890 and 1940. Those who carried out these attacks usually faced no legal consequences for their actions.

This map shows how widespread the practice was. Notice there were no recorded lynchings in Arlington County in this period, but all residents would have heard about lynchings in nearby towns. These events certainly would have shaped relations between African Americans and whites in Arlington. Some victims of lynchings had been accused of serious crimes, but many others were simply guilty of not knowing their “place” — being too ambitious, challenging a white person’s authority, or advocating for their own rights.
