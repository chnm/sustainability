Dr. Benjamin Vaughan to Dr. Benjamin Page
Hallowell, Sept. 26, 1800

I find Mr. Merrick's child better in all respects, except that the puffiness of the hands & feet extends over the rest of the body. This resembles the puffiness in the blacksmith's face, for both parties have it while recovering. I have given teira ponderosa to each. I consider the habit of body as so entirely changed with the child (for it has lost its stools,) that I shall recommend change of living; namely to meat and port wine.

Upon Sydenham's idea of dysentery being a fever turned inwards (fibris introversay) & upon mine, that instead of a general affection of the arterial system, there is an affection of this system in the bowels chiefly; I wish you would <{ mwSetSsv('answer', 'vaughan') }>apply opium & the digitalis to the patient at Mr. West's. The combination renders the opium diaphoretic, & perhaps a diuretic tendency remains in the fox glove; neither of which effects promise ill in dysentery, were it only upon the idea of revulsion being produced from the presence of these new actions in the system.

I send you some powders consisting of <{ mwSetSsv('answer', 'vaughan') }>foxglove & opium. The former came to Dr. Jafferys of Boston from Apothecaries Hall. The medicine was made up for Miss Hallowell, being the same which she had been in habit of taking with such palliative success. I think the powders may be divided into halves. The foxglove is in greatest quantity; perhaps as 2 to 1 [ ].
