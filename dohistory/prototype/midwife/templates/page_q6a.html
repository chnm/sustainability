Documents do not explicitly address all the questions/issues we would like them to. Detective work is required to distill the answers asked of them: we need to infer conclusions and make calculations from what is written. Even seemingly basic facts such as when was Martha Ballard born are revealed by inference from entries about a birthday. These rudimentary facts provide an anchor for more complicated questions. For instance, from calculating the number of births Martha Ballard attended each year, we learn that her midwifery practice slows down after 1800. Initially, Laurel Ulrich was puzzled by the sudden decline. Was the decline due to Martha's age? Were the doctors taking over her practice? Was she ill? Ulrich eventually pieced together the answer to the puzzle by discovering when the Ballards moved and by figuring out the location of the new house. By noting that the decline in deliveries occurred shortly after moving into the new house we can infer one of the causes of the decline: the new house was too remote and therefore it was difficult for people to call on her and difficult for her to get to patients (and women in labor) in time.

Many historians who had seen Martha Ballard's diary had brushed it aside believing that it was of little historical interest. That isn't true, but it took lots of hard work -- detective work -- to piece together her world.
