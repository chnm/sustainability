In some ways, Hammond’s language is far from cold. He clearly has strong feelings — about his "blood" and about his blood being owned by "Strangers." Only his son Harry, also his blood, can be trusted to "appreciate & and most independently carry out my wishes." Hammond’s language suggests the ways that the sexualized, racialized language of "blood" mixed with the commercial context of slave owning to help create a potent cultural brew supporting slavery in the South.

Further sources on the complexity of black-white sexual relations in the slave South include Harriet Jacobs’ 185? autobiographical account Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl, as well as the work of several historians who have shown that while whites deplored "miscegenation," it was in fact widespread. Historians have also explored how much whites structured the idea of "race" by their daily behavior.
