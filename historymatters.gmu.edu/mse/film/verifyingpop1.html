The physical properties of an original strip of film, apart from the images it contains, can reveal a great deal of information to film archivists and historians. For example, early film companies made films with slight differences in the shape of the sprocket holes or the nature of the frame lines. Many also placed trademarks or other information on the edge of the film. Unfortunately, film stock before the post-World War II era was manufactured with a nitrate base, creating an unstable film that deteriorated after sixty years or less. Most original films shot before World War II, therefore, have ceased to exist. Film archives, in an under-funded race against time, have worked to preserve early films by transferring them to Safety Film, but many films have been lost. In addition, archivists have discovered that later film stock (known as Safety or Acetate) also has severe preservation problems and has a very limited life if not properly stored.
