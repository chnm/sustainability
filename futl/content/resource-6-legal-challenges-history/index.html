Just the Beginning

The Arlington branch of the NAACP moved quickly to integrate Arlington schools after the Brown ruling. Several African American families and two white families petitioned the court to allow Arlington children to attend their neighborhood school regardless of color. As historian, James McGrath Morris notes, these families received frequent threatening phone calls, which caused one of the white families to drop out. In 1956, federal judge Albert Bryan ordered Arlington schools to desegregate, but also ordered any family seeking to register a child for school to use “all possible administrative channels” before bringing the complaint back to court.

The Virginia legislature passed the Pupil Placement Act that prevented African American students to immediately begin attending all-white schools. This Act required each student attending a new school to be reviewed by the state and effectively halted desegregation. As McGrath notes,

“From the point of view of the NAACP, the Pupil Placement Act was a devilishly clever ruse. Signed pupil placement forms had to be reviewed and processed and then sent back to the local school before the pupil could be enrolled. In other words, black students could be bureaucratically denied a place, but if they did not pursue this avenue, Judge Bryan would not consider a complaint.”

As a result, Arlington schools did not integrate in 1956. In fact, it would be three more years before African American students in Arlington were able to attend a previously all-white school. In response to the Brown ruling, the Virginia government focused on preventing desegregation. This policy of massive resistance threatened to close any school district in Virginia that tried to end segregation.
