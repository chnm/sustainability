description: Truxtun discusses frigate construction with McHenry.  He anticipated that the costs of the three frigates would be immense. Notes that he advised against building of naval armament to the southward
  of Philadelphia. Secretary Knox indicated that he believed that ships built to the southward would cost 25 percent more, but  President Washington was willing to experiment in order to ascertain where
  ships could be built under most favorable terms.  President also wanted to distribute money generally throughout the union. Goes on to say that Baltimore was not a good place for ship building. He had
  no choice of artificers, and laborers were scarce and indolent; prices too high. The master builder [Stodder], intended to make the most of the job by dragging it out, and declared he would never build
  another frigate. Truxtun proposed an alteration in authority, to no avail. Says that he saved the United States money, and never derived any advantage from ship building. Truxtun notes that he was not
  hesitant to challenge the agents in matters of accounts. Speaks highly of Sterrett; less so about Stodder.  Truxtun denies mishandling public monies; claims that David Stodder owes money to the United
  States.
