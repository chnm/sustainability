Although a number of cameramen followed the troops to Manila and documented the military campaign, an equal number of filmmakers remained in the U.S. and recreated key battles. The great distance and expense of sending cameramen to the Philippines led many filmmakers to stay home in New York and New Jersey, where the film industry then centered. Thus, in the first clip, Capture of Trenches at Candaba, the men fighting are actually members of the New Jersey National Guard, who were reenacting the battle in the Orange Mountains near West Orange, New Jersey.

The second clip, Advance of Kansas Volunteers at Caloocan, which is supposed to depict Colonel Frederick Funston and the 20th Kansas Infantry in a two-hour battle in which they emerged victorious, also features the New Jersey National Guard fighting in the Orange Mountains. Even if Edison had been willing to pay for sending his cameramen to the Philippines, they would not have been able to capture an actual battle with the kind of equipment then available to them. The cameraman's apparent position directly in the line of fire is also a clue that this clip does not depict a real battle.

The third clip, 25th Infantry, suggests what, in fact could be filmed live using the existing film equipment--a single static camera captures troops riding and marching past. Although the clip gives the copyright of May 21, 1902, it was actually filmed March 23, 1900, in the Philippine Islands, following the 25th Infantry's participation at the battle of Mt. Arayat in January 1900. The African-American troops of the 25th are led here by Gen. Frederick D. Grant and Gen. A. S. Burt.
