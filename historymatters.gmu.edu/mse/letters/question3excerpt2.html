Excerpt Two

On Monday — Dec. 4th I went to Port Jackson —; having bought a horse from a man named Cavert....Hugh Rusk, with whom I boarded, was a shrewd, successful planter — with a fine large lot of negro slaves —; he was certainly the most completely whisky soaked man I ever saw —, he was always brimful —; the first thing in the morning, & the last at night —; his nose & face blazed like a beacon —; reeking with it —, always boozy, but never more nor less so —; never drunk —, always shrewd & ready for business —; whisky never unfitted him for transacting business to his own advantage –; he had jet black hair & eyes, & as red a face as I ever saw — His wife was a young country girl —; her maiden name had been Snell....Port Jackson...was one of the worst, whisky drinking, fighting, horseracing, gambling communities, to be found this side of Texas, I suppose — in those days, rectified whisky only cost about 37 _ cts. a gallon, by the barrel —; and it was abundant —; there was a groggery [a small roadside tavern] about _ mile back from the Ferry, kept by a great fat beast of a man named Friday, that was always filled with tipplers —, and another one at the Ferry, a mile or so north of us, kept by a man named Bob Crawford —, which was a notorious resort for all of the riff raff of the country —;
