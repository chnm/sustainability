For Hammond, the women know the truth and, at least in this letter to his son, he is willing to accept as true a woman’s knowledge of who was the father of her children. He doesn’t want his son to doubt either woman. Such knowledge was thought to belong to white women as well, and thus Hammond is acknowledging both the veracity and the womanhood of Sally and Louisa — which, under law, they could not claim for themselves. What does this suggest about both the gap between master and slave and the ways the gap was not so wide in practice as it was in the "ideal" as represented by white law?

Where else would you look for evidence and information about this issue? Hammond’s wife, Catherine, left him at one point in protest of his relation with Louisa — a dramatic move for a wealthy and prominent woman in this society. You might want to read passages from Hammond’s personal diary to get a broader view of the man, or read the diaries of other slave owning men. You could also consult historical works on women and childbirth in the slave South, and historical accounts of the conflicted legal status of slaves as both persons and property.
