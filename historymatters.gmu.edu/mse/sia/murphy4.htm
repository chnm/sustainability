Well if you've got particularly a collection of letters you want to get the sequence right. You want to look for a date. Make sure you've got all the dates organized, and if there isn't a date there, spend some time trying to figure out if you can get as accurate a date as possible, because the sequence matters.

Someone who's reading any body of letters, but we can certainly use this as an example, wants to get more information on the events that are being discussed in the letter. So if, an anti-slavery campaign is being discussed, you would want to get more information on that. If a labor movement struggle for a ten hour day is being discussed, you would go and read newspapers on that. So, the first thing that you do is to try to find out as much as you can about the events being discussed in a letter. The second thing that a student would want to do, is to try to find out as much as he or she can, about the people who are involved in the communication. Either the person who is being written to, in this case Angelique Martin, a very important Fourierist and Feminist. Or as I'm interested in, the person who's writing the letter, Sarah, Bagley. I'm very interested in finding out what happened to her before. How she came out of the mills, and where she would be headed in the future. So trying to follow these individuals, into the past before the letters, and into the future after the letters, provides important contextualization.

Finally, it's extremely important for students who are writing about these letters to not get totally lost in the past. These documents, like any historical documents, mostly take on their significance when they enter into a dialogue with the work of other scholars on that period. So it's very important, for me, to be looking at what other scholars have said about the women's movement and the labor movement, and to take these letters and try to see if what I'm finding here tells us something that no other scholars knew. Am I going to reshape how scholars are writing about a particular movement. So you always want to be aware of what other scholars are writing. That's secondary scholarship. And, what you write has to be in dialogue with what they are writing.
