In one sense, Hammond coldly reasons on the economic realities of his slave ownership. Don’t forget, he’s been using his own children as his slave labor. What does this suggest about a planter’s economic opportunities stemming from his own sexual behavior? Note, too, that even in this confidential letter to his free son, Hammond balances the books: his "indulgence" of these few slaves (that is, he’s not going to subject them to sale; perhaps he also intends to give them lighter work), he reasons, will be "compensated" by "the services of the rest" of his slave labor force. Always the businessman.

You could find more evidence and information on this question in Hammond’s plantation diary — a kind of log of his cotton production — where he kept track of his slave labor force’s production and health, as well as noting the plantation’s "mulatto" children. Other plantation owners kept similar logs that reflect the way that owners treated slaves simply as property and labor. There are many historical studies that examine such sources, including studies of plantation discipline and management and studies of slave resistance. Other historians have studied slave trading more generally – from the sales between neighbors to city auction sales.
